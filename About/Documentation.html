1. The Topic of the Exhibition: Apollonian and Dionysian

The choice of the theme of this exhibition is situated within the broader framework of the dichotomies that permeate human experience and that have long been used to give form and meaning to the complexity of existence. The concepts of the Apollonian and the Dionysian refer to a primordial principle, comparable to the ancient opposition between chaos and cosmos, through which past cultures sought to explain the origin and order of the universe.
These two forces do not represent a moral opposition between good and evil, but rather a dynamic tension between complementary poles: irrational and rational, instinct and measure, disorder and harmony, intoxication and control. They describe different, yet equally necessary, ways through which human beings perceive, interpret, and create the world. It is no coincidence that in Greek religion, at the roots of Western spirituality and imagination, two deities came to embody these fundamental principles, which govern life and sustain its balance.
Apollo represents the principle of form, clarity, and measure. As the god of light, music, poetry, and prophecy, he embodies the ideal of a harmonious and rational order founded on proportion and knowledge. The Apollonian is the realm of luminous appearance, composed beauty, and the intellect that seeks to impose structure on reality. It is the force inclined toward control, definition, and stability, finding expression in artistic forms grounded in balance, symmetry, and formal clarity.
Dionysus, by contrast, embodies vital energy in its most instinctive and overwhelming form. As the god of wine, intoxication, theatre, and metamorphosis, he represents the dissolution of individual boundaries, the abandonment to sensory experience, and the fusion with the primordial rhythm of nature. The Dionysian is excess, impulse, and dismeasure; it is the force that breaks apparent order to reveal a deeper truth rooted in emotion, corporeality, and collective participation. Through Dionysus, life manifests itself as continuous flow and incessant transformation.
It was the philosopher Friedrich Nietzsche who systematically articulated this opposition, identifying the Apollonian and the Dionysian as the two original forces of art and Western culture. In his thought, authentic art arises precisely from the tension and interaction between these principles, from the ability to hold together form and impulse, measure and intoxication, without one negating the other. Far from being separable, the Apollonian and the Dionysian intertwine, contaminate one another, and undergo constant transformation.
It is therefore unsurprising that countless works of art, originating from different periods, places, and artistic languages, engage with these themes, sometimes emphasizing one pole, sometimes blending them in complex and ambiguous ways. Painting, sculpture, music, literature, and the performing arts have continuously reworked this fundamental tension, making it visible in both the forms and the contents of artistic creation.
The Tyrsos & Lyre Mouseion was conceived precisely from these reflections. The exhibition presents works drawn from heterogeneous cultural contexts and different historical periods, united by the explicit or underlying presence of one or both of these principles. The aim is not to rigidly classify the works, but to offer visitors an interpretive framework that highlights how the Apollonian and the Dionysian continue to operate as structuring forces within the human imagination.

2. The Collection

The current collection, which can also be visited physically until 30 February 2027 at the evocative Temple of Apollo, within the Sanctuary of Delphi at the foot of Mount Parnassus, brings together a heterogeneous group of works spanning an exceptionally broad chronological range. The exhibited artifacts extend from the Greco-Roman period to the contemporary era, offering visitors a journey across different epochs, artistic languages, and cultural contexts, all united by the presence of Apollonian and Dionysian principles.
The selection includes objects of varied nature and function. Alongside everyday items, such as cups used for the symposium, a ritual moment closely associated with the Dionysian sphere, there are theatrical masks, which refer to the world of stage performance and the dual dimension of tragedy and comedy.
The collection also includes statues and paintings depicting Apollo and Dionysus, as well as written texts, theoretical treatises, and musical scores that, since antiquity, have contributed to articulating, questioning, and at times problematizing this fundamental dichotomy.
Architecture and the representation of society are likewise present within the exhibition path. Some paintings depict the city through radically opposing visions: on the one hand, the orderly, rational, and ideal city; on the other, the dynamic, chaotic, and pulsating city.
As can also be observed through the Map available in The Museum section, the objects have been arranged according to a precise spatial criterion within the temple’s chambers. On the right side of the exhibition route are concentrated the artifacts primarily associated with the Apollonian principle, while those related to the Dionysian principle are placed on the left side.
The works are presented in pairs of opposites, selected to allow visitors a direct comparison between objects linked by a shared thematic thread but oriented toward different poles of the dichotomy.

3. The Logo

The Association’s logo, visible on the left side of the website’s navigation bar, constitutes a visual synthesis of the conceptual principles underlying the exhibition and the identity of the Tyrsos & Lyre Mouseion.
The name Tyrsos & Lyre explicitly refers to these two symbolic objects. The thyrsus, a ritual staff wrapped in ivy and topped with a pinecone, is one of the most recognizable emblems of Dionysus. The lyre, a musical instrument traditionally attributed to Apollo, embodies the opposite and complementary principle: harmony, measure, proportion, and the ability to transform chaos into order through sound.
From a formal perspective, the logo combines these two elements into a unified and balanced composition. The lyre is depicted through vertical and symmetrical lines, while the thyrsus introduces organic and ascending movement. Above the lyre is placed a stylized sun, a direct reference to Apollo as the god of light and clarity, reinforcing the Apollonian dimension of reason, illumination, and formal balance. The presence of the sun visually anchors the lyre to the principle of luminosity and knowledge, completing the symbolic triad that defines the logo.
The use of a restrained color palette and an essential graphic style further reinforces the institutional character of the logo, clearly expressing its cultural mission: to explore the relationship between the Apollonian and the Dionysian as an ongoing dialogue.

4. The Aesthetic and Stylistic Choices

Apollonian and Dionysian aesthetics have a well-established stylistic perception in the collective imagination. For this reason, the design of this website aims to reflect this shared perception, which is also evident in the art historically associated with these two archetypes. The primary visual approach of the site is therefore divided into Dark and Light modes, representing Dionysus and Apollo respectively. Users can switch between these modes via a toggle, implemented using JavaScript that updates the CSS custom properties defined in :root.
The CSS design has been carefully crafted to support this dual aesthetic. In Dark Mode (Dionysian), the palette is dominated by deep, muted tones—#2a2422 for backgrounds, warm highlights like #F4C430, and accent colors such as #9c2844 and #641527. This choice conveys intensity, mystery, and expressiveness, evoking the emotional and immersive qualities associated with Dionysus. Light Mode (Apollonian), in contrast, uses soft neutrals and warm shades—#F3E9DA for the background, #eba311 for titles, and #8B3A2F for links—promoting clarity, harmony, and balance, in line with Apollo’s rational and ordered aesthetic. Typography reinforces this duality: the serif font Cardo is used as the main text font, providing readability and classical elegance, while Cinzel is reserved for headings to communicate gravitas and formality. Font sizes are thoughtfully scaled across devices, ensuring hierarchy, legibility, and a consistent reading rhythm. For example, base font size is 1.2em with responsive adjustments for smaller screens, and headings are proportionally sized to emphasize content importance. Other stylistic details, such as button shapes, modal overlays, and hover effects, have been designed to complement the color palettes and enhance interactivity. The theme switch itself uses visual metaphors—☽ for night (Dionysian) and ☼ for day (Apollonian)—while smooth transitions, gradients, and shadows create a cohesive, polished user experience.
Below is an overview of the main stylistic choices made for the website, detailing colors, typography, and design rationale for both Apollonian (Light) and Dionysian (Dark) aesthetics.

Color Palette

  • Text (--text): #B8ADA7 — soft off-white for readability against dark tones.

5. The Home Page

The homepage of Tyrsos & Lyre is built around simplicity and visual impact, drawing inspiration from major museum websites like the British Museum. At the top, the hero section features Caravaggio’s Bacchus, positioned on the left according to the golden ratio. This placement leaves the center for the title “Tyrsos & Lyre” and an empty space to the right, creating balance and breathing room. The user is thus invited to take a glass of wine, immediately setting the immersive atmosphere of the symposium, and is then welcomed to the exhibition with a Nietzsche quotation on the Apollonian and Dionysian.

Below the hero, a brief introduction guides the visitor. Conceived as the only purely textual part, it provides just the essential information, keeping the focus on images and a few carefully chosen elements.

The homepage then presents two main sections as horizontal cards: “The Narratives” and “The Museum.” Each pairs an image with a link, making navigation intuitive while letting visuals speak for themselves.

The collection section allows users to explore freely through a horizontal scroller, dynamically populated with JavaScript using a JSON file. Clicking on any object opens a modal with metadata, enabling deeper exploration without leaving the page. A ticket booking section follows, featuring a simple form and a confirmation modal to complete the experience.

Navigation is always available via a fixed-top navbar, which includes options to switch visual themes and toggle between dark and light modes. The footer, large and comprehensive, is organized into three columns with practical information, links to all main sections, and details about the project and institution, with a rights note placed at the very bottom.

Overall, the homepage balances minimal text, large visual sections, and interactive elements, guiding the visitor through the exhibition in a clear, immersive, and visually engaging way, while leaving space to explore freely at their own pace.

7. The Exhibition Page

The Exhibition page constitutes the core of the virtual museum experience, presenting the complete set of works included in the Tyrsos & Lyre Mouseion. Conceived as a digital gallery, the page allows visitors to explore the collection as a unified corpus, without predefined interpretive paths, thus offering a direct encounter with the artworks.
The layout is structured as a grid of eighteen items, each represented by an image and a concise descriptive text. By selecting an artwork, visitors can access additional information through an interactive modal window, which provides extended descriptions and metadata. This design choice emphasizes visual engagement while maintaining clarity and accessibility, encouraging free navigation across different periods, media, and thematic orientations.
The Exhibition page reflects the conceptual axis of the Apollonian and the Dionysian not through explicit categorization, but through juxtaposition. By placing works associated with order, harmony, and measure alongside those expressing excess, transformation, and emotional intensity, the page invites users to reflect on the coexistence and tension between these principles within the history of art.

8. The Narratives Page

The Narratives page offers an alternative mode of exploration based on interpretive frameworks rather than on the complete display of the collection. Its purpose is to guide visitors through the artworks by proposing thematic and conceptual paths that foreground specific aspects of the Apollonian–Dionysian dichotomy.
Two main narrative approaches are presented: one based on symbolic figures, focusing on Apollo and Dionysus as archetypal forces, and another organized around artistic domains, such as performing arts, visual arts, and literature. These narratives allow users to approach the same collection from different perspectives, highlighting how recurring ideas and tensions manifest across diverse artistic languages.
By structuring the experience through narratives, the page emphasizes interpretation, comparison, and critical reflection. Rather than offering a linear or hierarchical reading of the works, it encourages visitors to construct their own understanding of the exhibition, reinforcing the idea that meaning emerges through the dynamic interaction between order and excess, structure and transformation.

9. The Themes

The Themes section allows visitors to explore the exhibition through stylistic and cultural frameworks rather than through chronology or individual artworks. Each theme corresponds to a specific aesthetic environment that reinterprets the Apollonian–Dionysian dichotomy by translating it into visual language, atmosphere, and interaction design.

9.3 La Belle Époque

Inspired by the Belle Époque, this theme conveys mystery, elegance, and poetic decadence. The background blends deep bottle green (#132018) into near-black (#0b0c0b), while text is warm ivory (#e8dfc9) for readability. Headings and titles use muted olive green (#3a4f2c) and golden highlights (#d1b24a), while rich reds (#b21f2d for links, #7b1b23 for emphasized text) punctuate the layout. Borders and modals incorporate subtle glows (#F4D06F) and semi-transparent dark backgrounds (rgba(15,13,12,0.96)) for coherence.

The hero section features a Chat Noir–inspired image under a gradient overlay, with expressive typography and a circular “absinthe bottle” button referencing the decadent motifs of the era. The composition balances color, texture, and interactive elements to immerse visitors in a world of elegance, mystery, and Belle Époque drama.

9.4 Art Deco

The Art Deco theme emphasizes order, symmetry, and formal elegance, aligning predominantly with the Apollonian principle. Inspired by the visual culture of the early twentieth century and literary imaginaries such as The Great Gatsby, this theme conveys a sense of controlled luxury, geometric clarity, and aesthetic refinement. The structured layouts, restrained color palette, and decorative motifs evoke a world in which excess is disciplined by form, and pleasure is framed within precise architectural and visual boundaries.

At the same time, the presence of Dionysian elements is not entirely suppressed. Beneath the polished surface, the theme hints at underlying desires, ambition, and social performance. The Art Deco aesthetic thus becomes a visual expression of the tension between measured harmony and latent excess, reflecting the fragile balance between Apollonian control and Dionysian impulse.

9.2 Psychedelic 70s

The Psychedelic 70s theme foregrounds the Dionysian dimension of the exhibition through vibrant colors, fluid forms, and immersive visual effects. Drawing inspiration from the culture of nightclubs, discotheques, and collective dance experiences, this theme emphasizes sensory overload, rhythm, and altered perception. The intense chromatic palette and dynamic graphic elements create an atmosphere of movement and emotional intensity.

In this context, the Dionysian principle manifests as loss of individual boundaries and immersion in a collective experience driven by music and bodily presence. Apollonian structure is deliberately weakened, appearing only in residual forms that prevent complete visual chaos. The resulting aesthetic mirrors the tension between freedom and form, echoing the ecstatic yet transient nature of the disco culture of the 1970s.

9.6 90s Games

This theme merges the bold, chaotic energy of 90s video games with a sense of nostalgic futurism. The color palette is central to this aesthetic. The deep indigo background (#0A0A50), overlaid with subtle gradients, creates mystery and depth reminiscent of late-night arcade screens or neon-lit streets. Neon accents dominate the visual language: cyan (#00FFEA) for main text ensures legibility, luminous green (#39FF14) for titles pops immediately, yellow (#FFFF00) highlights guide the eye, and bold magentas (#FF00FF and #FF1493) bring intensity to links, borders, and interactive elements.

The hero section features Bacchus, “Bac-man,” generated with ChatGPT, layered under a gradient that fades to black to enhance depth. The scrolling crawl animation, adapted and modified from CSS-Tricks, creates a retro, theatrical introduction.

Navigation bars, buttons, and modals follow the same neon, high-contrast logic. Deep blues and purples form the background canvas, while glowing borders, overlays, and hover effects reinforce the arcade and retro-futuristic vibe. The combination of colors and the layered hero image creates movement and energy, evoking the excitement of booting up a game or watching a 90s cinematic opening.

10. Technical Infrastructure

The project is organized around a modular file structure. Each web page is composed of multiple HTML files.

The default styling is handled globally through a single main.css file, supported by a shared external JavaScript file (common.js) used for common functionalities across pages. The Museum section and the Homepage rely on additional external JavaScript files for their specific behaviors, whereas the narrative-related pages do not use page-specific external scripts.

Object descriptions and metadata displayed in the Exhibition section of the Homepage are dynamically populated inside modals via an external JavaScript file (scripts/home.js) in combination with a data.json file. Visual themes are managed through separate CSS files stored in the Themes folder on GitHub, each building upon main.css with customized variations. All images are stored in the imgs directory, with the exception of the 1970s and Art Deco themes, which use dedicated image folders.

In general, external files are used whenever reusability, maintainability, or dynamic behavior is required, while inline code is kept to a minimum and only used in specific cases such as the About pages, where few and highly repetitive elements made inline CSS a simpler and more efficient choice.
