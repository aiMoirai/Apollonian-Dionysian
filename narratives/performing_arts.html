Tragic mask

Plaque depicting a female tragic theatrical mask.

This small bone plaque comes from late Republican Rome or the early decades of the Empire and depicts a female tragic mask, a central motif in ancient theatre. Masks, in both Greece and Rome, were used to make characters immediately recognizable and to convey strong emotions even to spectators seated far from the stage, turning the face into a universal symbol of pathos and drama. In this sense, tragedy was not merely entertainment, but a moment of reflection on the human condition, on fate, and on the passions that drive action.
The plaque, smaller than a real mask, has a pierced hole suggesting it was used decoratively on furniture or other domestic objects, rather than as an actual stage element. The female face, with gathered hair and a diadem, evokes mythological figures or tragic characters of high status, while the pronounced features – large eyes, downturned mouth, and arched eyebrows – make the suffering and pathos immediately perceptible. The material, carefully worked bone, demonstrates Roman craftsmanship in transforming a commonplace object into a cultural and theatrical symbol: in this way, a small domestic object condensed the essence of theatrical drama, bringing the grandeur and evocative power of the public stage into private life.

Comic mask

Comic theatrical mask from Magna Graecia, attributed to Italic farce.

The mask is one of the most significant symbols of ancient Italy’s comic theatre. Italic farces — derived from the Greek phlyakes — were short popular performances of a satirical and grotesque nature, born in Southern Italy during the Magna Graecia period. Through comedy and the exaggeration of features, they staged everyday life, vices, fears, and human weaknesses, serving as an ironic mirror of society.
The mask, preserved in the National Archaeological Museum of Taranto (MArTA), is a terracotta artifact dating to around the 2nd–1st century BCE, originating from a funerary context in the area of Taranto, an ancient city of Magna Graecia. It was discovered during excavations near a necropolis, where it likely formed part of the burial goods of an actor or a theatre enthusiast. The piece depicts a highly expressive male face, with a wide-open mouth, pronounced nose, arched eyebrows, and bulging eyes — features typical of comic and farcical characters. The modelling is lively and deliberately exaggerated, intended to be recognizable even from a distance during performances. From a symbolic perspective, the mask reflects the connection between the world of the living and that of the dead: theatre, a place of fiction and transformation, becomes a metaphor for life itself, always suspended between reality and illusion. This unique artifact bears witness to the importance of the Italic farce as an artistic and social expression in Hellenistic Taranto, a city where Greek and local cultures blended to create one of antiquity’s most refined theatrical traditions.

Kylix with Apollo and kithara

Attic red-figure kylix (480-470 BC). The interior depicts Apollo, identifiable as the god of music, seated and playing the kithara, his symbolic instrument, beside a flaming altar. The exterior shows libation scenes with male and female figures, sceptres, and phialai, enriched with decorative motifs such as meanders and palmettes.

Red-figure kylix created circa 480 BC-470 BC. The interior depicts Apollo, beardless, with long wavy hair, a laurel wreath, and a himation around his legs, seated to the right on a diphros. The god plays a large kithara and holds a fluted phiale in his right hand. On the right, half-seen, is a flaming altar, shaped like an Ionic column with necking of egg pattern.
The exterior depicts a woman in a long-sleeved chiton, himation, and saccos, who moves to right with left hand on her hip, holding out in her right an oinochoe, as if to pour into a phiale which a bearded, wreathed man on right holds out to her. He has a long sceptre leaning against his left shoulder, and wears a long-sleeved talaric chiton and himation. On the left, a youth, wreathed, and wearing an himation, holding upright a sceptre in his left, and extending a fluted phiale in his right. Between him and the woman is a slender Ionic column with architrave; behind him hang a pair of sandals. On the left of the bearded man hang a pair of tablets rolled up, with a stylus fastened in the bands: at each of the lower corners hangs a triple tie.
On the other side, apparently the same three figures are engaged, but part of each is missing. Of the youth on left only the feet and the ends of his himation and the legs of the chair in which he is sitting to right are preserved. The woman confronts him, raising both hands apparently, with a gesture of surprise; she wears a fillet with trefoil ends; her face and right arm are missing. Behind her is the column as we can see on the other side. The bearded man on the right stands en face, but looking to left with right hand on hip, left holding sceptre upright; his sceptre is striated. On the right is a trace of an object (a sandal ?) hanging up, which is almost entirely broken away.
Purple tuning pegs of lyre, flames, wreaths in exterior, fillets, cords of tablets and of sandals. Eye in transition stage (in interior, the eyelids are in profile; the eyeball is drawn as a dot in a semicircle). Around the interior, a band of running maeander. Below the exterior, a thin red line. Below the handles a palmette. The exergue in the interior is left red.
