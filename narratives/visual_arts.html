Apollo of the Belvedere

Apollo of the Belvedere, Roman statue of the 2nd century AD, modeled as a copy of a Greek original. The statue was discovered in Rome in 1489 and acquired by Cardinal Giuliano della Rovere, later Pope Julius II, who moved it to the Vatican. Admired since its placement in the Belvedere, it was praised by Winckelmann as the highest ideal of ancient Greek art.

The Apollo of the Belvedere is one of the most celebrated works of classical art and for centuries was regarded as the supreme embodiment of ideal beauty. Its fame, consolidated from the Renaissance onward, made it a symbol of formal perfection and Greek harmony, inspiring artists, theorists, and travelers. Although it is a Roman marble copy from the 2nd century CE, the sculpture preserves the elegance and expressive power of the presumed Greek bronze original attributed to Leochares. Its history, meaning, and iconography form a complex framework that reveals its central role in European artistic culture; these aspects become clearer when examining the circumstances of its discovery, its original function, and the symbolic system it encapsulates.
More specifically, the statue was discovered at the end of the fifteenth century, probably in the area of Anzio or Grottaferrata, and was immediately acquired by the popes, who placed it in the Belvedere Courtyard, where it became a fundamental model for Renaissance artists. The Greek bronze original was most likely a celebratory work created for a sanctuary dedicated to Apollo, while the Roman copy reflects the collecting and decorative tastes of the imperial elite. Iconographically, Apollo is depicted at the moment after releasing an arrow: his body stretches into a dynamic and balanced pose, his head gently turned, his gaze raised, with quiver and cloak completing the god’s heroic aspect. Iconologically, the statue conveys the principles of harmony and rationality associated with Apollo in classical culture: the idealized body does not narrate an episode but embodies cosmic order, intellectual purity, and moral perfection. This symbolic dimension made the sculpture an absolute model, so much so that Winckelmann described it as “the highest ideal of art among ancient works,” thereby securing its place in the imagination of modern Europe.

Bacchus by Caravaggio

Caravaggio’s Bacchus was rediscovered in 1913 and later dated to around 1596, during the artist’s years under Cardinal del Monte. It belongs to his early half-length figure paintings and shows a young Bacchus offering wine and fruit. Critics have read irony in the depiction, suggesting Bacchus resembles a languid Roman tavern boy rather than a classical god. The still life elements (especially the fruit basket) carry symbolic weight, possibly alluding to a Horatian invitation to moderation, conviviality, and friendship. Some scholars also emphasize the sensual and homoerotic undertones of the scene, linking the work to ancient rites and ideas of physical freedom.

Caravaggio’s Bacchus, rediscovered in 1913 and dated to around 1596, belongs to the artist’s early years under the patronage of Cardinal Francesco Maria del Monte. The painting is part of his early half-length works and depicts a youthful Bacchus offering wine and fruit to the viewer, establishing a direct, almost intimate relationship with the beholder.
Far from classical idealization, the god appears as a real young man, with a soft body and a languid gaze—closer to a tavern boy than to an Olympian deity. This choice lends the painting a subtle irony and a strong naturalistic charge, characteristic of the early Caravaggio. The still life in the foreground, with ripe fruit already marked by decay, introduces a symbolic dimension: a reminder of the transience of pleasure and of the carpe diem ethos often associated with the poetry of Horace.
Alongside this moral undertone, the Dionysian element emerges with particular force. The wine, the offering gesture, and the relaxed posture evoke intoxication as an experience of freedom, the suspension of rules, and sensorial knowledge. The sensuality of the image recalls ancient Dionysian rites and an idea of instinctive, earthly vitality. Bacchus thus appears as an ambiguous and compelling image, poised between everyday reality and myth, restraint and excess, realism and pagan ritual.

Apollo and the Muses on Mount Parnassus

Nicolas Poussin’s drawing depicts Apollo playing a lira di braccio, surrounded by the nine Muses and figures like Homer and Virgil along the Castalian spring at Mount Parnassus. Inspired by Raphael’s Vatican fresco via Raimoni’s engraving, Apollo is placed slightly left of center, with the Muses arranged semi-circularly to guide the viewer’s eye. The combination of soft washes and fine pen lines creates a lively, three-dimensional sense of movement and depth.

This drawing by Nicolas Poussin (French, 1594–1665) depicts an animated mythological scene in which the nine Muses, goddesses of poetic inspiration and the arts, surround Apollo, who is shown playing a lira di braccio. Homer and Virgil are present, gathered alongside the Castalian Spring, a legendary source of inspiration and learning. The spring flows through the center of the composition, while Mount Parnassus, celebrated for its beauty, forms the backdrop, creating a setting ideal for music and contemplation.
Poussin’s composition is full of movement, achieved both through the dynamic arrangement of figures and his lively drawing style. The design draws on an engraving by Marcantonio Raimondi after Raphael’s Parnassus fresco in the Vatican, though Poussin positions Apollo slightly left of center. The Muses are arranged in a semi-circular formation, leading the viewer’s eye around the scene and upward through the trees to the putti hovering in the sky.
The work combines soft washes with fine pen lines, creating a delicate interplay of light and shadow that imparts a sense of three-dimensionality and vitality. Though not currently on view, the drawing remains a remarkable example of Poussin’s engagement with classical mythology and his skill in translating grand, narrative compositions into lively, detailed works on paper.

Bacchus and Satyr by Michelangelo

Sculpture of Bacco and a Satyr by Michelangelo Buonarroti, a masterful study where classical form meets expressive realism.

Michelangelo's Bacchus was created by the young genius in his twenties during his first stay in Rome and portrays the god of wine as he had never been seen before: caught in a moment of swaying drunkenness, his perfect body tilts in a sensual and perilous balance. More than an Olympian god, he resembles an adolescent troubled by his own strength and passions. It is in this audacity, in this break with the pure classical ideal, that its hypnotic power and modernity reside.
Upon closer analysis, all the elements of Michelangelo's mastery emerge. Commissioned by a Florentine aristocrat and currently located in the Bargello Museum, the Bacchus (1496-1497) depicts the god as he sways unsteadily, cup in hand, accompanied by a satyr who underscores his wild nature. The revolution lies in the fusion of an impeccable anatomical study—rendering every muscle and tendon—with an unprecedented psychological introspection. His gaze is, in fact, lost and ambiguous, his smile drunken and mischievous. Michelangelo is not merely imitating classical models; he is subverting them from within: drawing inspiration from Hellenistic ephebes, he infuses them with a physical and emotional realism that is entirely Renaissance. Iconographically, inebriation is conveyed through posture and interactions. Iconologically, the work becomes a profound investigation into the human condition, exploring the duality between the body's perfection and its fragility, between ecstasy and transience. It is this ingenious synthesis that makes it a timeless masterpiece.

The Ideal City of Urbino

The famous Ideal City of Urbino, attribution uncertain: a masterful vision of Renaissance harmony and proportion.

The Ideal City of Urbino represents one of the highest examples of Renaissance culture. This painting, created in the circle of Piero della Francesca by an as-yet unidentified artist in the late fifteenth century, was born in the cultured court of Federico da Montefeltro, where architects and theorists like Leon Battista Alberti developed the principles of rational urban planning.
The work does not depict a real city, but rather the image of a perfect city as it must have appeared to the humanists. Its harmonious architecture, orderly squares, and rigorous perspective create a balanced and rational whole. The absence of human figures is not accidental but serves to concentrate attention on the beauty of the silent architecture and the ideal of an orderly and measured society. The central perspective, perfectly calculated, is not merely a technique but a symbol of a worldview that places man at the center of a measurable and ordered space. This painting can be considered a true manifesto of Renaissance culture, expressing through architectural forms the ideal of a city where harmony and rationality reign— fundamental values for the formation of the ideal citizen. The work thus embodies the utopia of balance triumphing over chaos, reflecting the ambition of the Urbino court to become a model of enlightened governance and aesthetic perfection.

The City Rises by Umberto Boccioni

The City Rises by Umberto Boccioni, a 1910 painting, symbolizes the chaotic movement of the modern city under construction.

The City Rises is one of the most significant masterpieces of Italian Futurism, painted by Umberto Boccioni between 1910 and 1911. With this work, the artist breaks decisively from traditional painting and proposes a new vision of reality — dynamic, energetic, and alive. The painting depicts the birth of the modern industrial city, where men, horses, and machines merge into a single whirlwind of motion. Through diagonal brushstrokes and vibrant colors, Boccioni conveys the sense of movement and enthusiasm that accompanies urban progress, transforming the scene into an intense and overwhelming visual experience. The image does not merely represent a construction site but becomes a symbol of the vital force of modernity, of humankind building its own future with drive and confidence. In the background, cranes, scaffolding, and buildings under construction evoke early twentieth-century Milan, a rapidly developing city and emblem of the new industrial civilization. In the foreground, human and animal figures dissolve into light, losing their contours and expressing the fusion between man, machine, and city. The use of warm tones such as red and orange, contrasted with cooler, metallic hues, creates a vibrating effect that enhances the sensation of energy and transformation. From an iconographic and symbolic point of view, the work celebrates the Futurist faith in progress, speed, and the continuous metamorphosis of the modern world, standing as a true pictorial manifesto of the new age that was emerging.

Kylix with Apollo and kithara

Attic red-figure kylix (480-470 BC). The interior depicts Apollo, identifiable as the god of music, seated and playing the kithara, his symbolic instrument, beside a flaming altar. The exterior shows libation scenes with male and female figures, sceptres, and phialai, enriched with decorative motifs such as meanders and palmettes.

Red-figure kylix created circa 480 BC-470 BC. The interior depicts Apollo, beardless, with long wavy hair, a laurel wreath, and a himation around his legs, seated to the right on a diphros. The god plays a large kithara and holds a fluted phiale in his right hand. On the right, half-seen, is a flaming altar, shaped like an Ionic column with necking of egg pattern.
The exterior depicts a woman in a long-sleeved chiton, himation, and saccos, who moves to right with left hand on her hip, holding out in her right an oinochoe, as if to pour into a phiale which a bearded, wreathed man on right holds out to her. He has a long sceptre leaning against his left shoulder, and wears a long-sleeved talaric chiton and himation. On the left, a youth, wreathed, and wearing an himation, holding upright a sceptre in his left, and extending a fluted phiale in his right. Between him and the woman is a slender Ionic column with architrave; behind him hang a pair of sandals. On the left of the bearded man hang a pair of tablets rolled up, with a stylus fastened in the bands: at each of the lower corners hangs a triple tie.
On the other side, apparently the same three figures are engaged, but part of each is missing. Of the youth on left only the feet and the ends of his himation and the legs of the chair in which he is sitting to right are preserved. The woman confronts him, raising both hands apparently, with a gesture of surprise; she wears a fillet with trefoil ends; her face and right arm are missing. Behind her is the column as we can see on the other side. The bearded man on the right stands en face, but looking to left with right hand on hip, left holding sceptre upright; his sceptre is striated. On the right is a trace of an object (a sandal ?) hanging up, which is almost entirely broken away.
Purple tuning pegs of lyre, flames, wreaths in exterior, fillets, cords of tablets and of sandals. Eye in transition stage (in interior, the eyelids are in profile; the eyeball is drawn as a dot in a semicircle). Around the interior, a band of running maeander. Below the exterior, a thin red line. Below the handles a palmette. The exergue in the interior is left red.

Eye-cup with Dionysus

Drinking cup created by the renowned potter and vase painter Exekias around 530 BC. Inside, it depicts Dionysus, the god of wine, reclining on a ship with a billowing white sail. Vines climb up the mast, while dolphins leap and dance around the vessel. On the exterior are the characteristic features that gave this type of cup its name: the eye-cup. Two large painted eyes decorate the surface; when drinking from the cup, they align with the drinker’s own, creating the illusion that the person has taken on new, typically Dionysian traits.

The black-figure drinking kylix (cup), created by the potter and vase painter Exekias around 530 BC, is one of the most famous ancient vessels of all time.
Inside you can see Dionysus, the god of wine, lying on a ship with a swelled white sail. Along the mast, vines climb up and dolphins dance around the ship. With this picture, the beholder is to be reminded of a well-known myth about the god: Dionysus had been captured by Etruscan pirates and abducted aboard their ship. On the high seas, however, the god appeared to the men in his true form, after which they were seized with panic and they plunged into the sea where Dionysus transformed them into dolphins.
Whoever drank from this bowl had a rather peculiar experience: for him, the god emerged from the sea of red wine, he floated on the waves for a brief moment and then he sailed his ship into the drinker’s open mouth. The bowl was therefore much more than just an extravagant drinking vessel. Through each sip, the user came closer to the god Dionysus, as it were.
Furthermore, on the exterior there are the characteristic features that gave this type of cup its name: the eye-cup. Two large painted eyes decorate the surface; when drinking from the cup, they align with the drinker's own, creating the illusion that the person has taken on new, typically Dionysian traits.
Exekias was well aware of the outstanding quality of the vessel he had created. He signed his work on the outside on the edge of the foot of the vessel: “Exekias epoiese” — Exekias made it.

Apollo and Daphne

Bernini depicts Apollo in mid-stride, his right foot grounded and his left suspended, the drapery flowing with his motion. As he reaches out to touch Daphne, the nymph, caught in her desperate flight, begins her transformation into a laurel tree: her fingers sprout leaves, her feet take root. Drawn from Ovid’s Metamorphoses, the myth becomes in Bernini’s hands a reflection on the tension between desire and freedom, on metamorphosis as both boundary and salvation, expressed through the vivid theatricality of the Baroque.

Gian Lorenzo Bernini’s Apollo and Daphne (1622–1625) captures the climactic moment of the tale told in Ovid’s Metamorphoses. Struck by Cupid’s golden arrow, Apollo is seized with passion for the nymph Daphne, follower of Diana. She, pierced by a lead arrow that inspires aversion, flees his pursuit and calls upon her father, the river god Peneus, to transform her and save her.
Bernini fixes the instant of this transformation: Apollo, running with his right foot touching the ground and his left raised, extends his hand to grasp Daphne. Under his divine touch, her escape freezes — her arms stretch upward, her fingers sprout into leaves, her hair turns to laurel, and her feet sink into the earth as roots.
The sculptor creates a powerfully theatrical scene: Apollo’s flowing drapery accentuates the motion, and the spiral composition compels the viewer to move around the sculpture, witnessing the metamorphosis unfold. Beyond its technical mastery and Baroque dynamism, the work reads the myth as an allegory of human desire and divine intervention, transforming Ovid’s narrative into a meditation on the limits of passion and the fragile balance between sensuality and transcendence.
