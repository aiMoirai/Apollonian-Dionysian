Dionysus

Dionysus appears as a force of excess, transformation, and ecstatic release, opposing rational control and fixed boundaries.

As god of wine, ritual madness, and collective frenzy, Dionysus embodies the dissolution of order and the suspension of individual identity. His power manifests through intoxication, music, dance, and emotional excess, opening the subject to transformation and communal experience.

The Triumph of Bacchus and Ariadne

In The Triumph of Bacchus and Ariadne, taken from Lorenzo de’ Medici’s Carnival Songs, the poet brings to life the mythical procession of Bacchus and Ariadne: Ariadne, abandoned on the island of Naxos, is found and taken as bride by the god of wine. The myth becomes a celebration of youth, pleasure, and festivity, but also an invitation to seize the moment, aware of life’s fleeting nature.

The Triumph of Bacchus and Ariadne, written by Lorenzo de’ Medici probably for the Carnival of 1490, belongs to his Carnival Songs (Canti Carnascialeschi), poems meant to be sung collectively during Florentine festivities.
In the text, the myth of Ariadne, the Cretan princess abandoned by the hero Theseus on the island of Naxos, is revisited and transformed: Ariadne is rescued and exalted by the god Bacchus, who parades triumphantly with his procession of satyrs, nymphs, and maenads. The tone is festive and playful, yet beneath it lies a deeper reflection: “How beautiful is youth, / which yet so quickly flies away!” («Quant’è bella giovinezza, / che si fugge tuttavia!») the refrain that becomes a moral lesson to live the present fully.
The use of myth here goes far beyond simple storytelling: Bacchus and Ariadne are not merely mythological figures, but symbols of beauty, love, sensuality, and vitality, all to be savored before time consumes them. The triumph of Bacchus is thus the triumph of pleasure, youth, and life itself, but also a reminder of their fragility and impermanence.
In the humanist culture of Lorenzo’s Florence, the myth takes on an allegorical meaning. The carnival becomes not just a time of revelry, but a reflection on transience and the human condition: Bacchus embodies the joy of living and the power of desire, while Ariadne, once abandoned and then redeemed, represents renewal through love. Together they express a Renaissance carpe diem: an invitation to embrace life joyfully, with both awareness and passion, before it fades away.

Dionysian Ritual

A visual evocation of ritual ecstasy, collective trance, and loss of control.

Dionysian rituals suspend social and individual boundaries, allowing participants to merge into a shared emotional experience. Music, dance, and intoxication function as tools of transformation, dissolving order and affirming instinct.
