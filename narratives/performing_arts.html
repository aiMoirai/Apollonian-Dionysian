The Well-Tempered Clavier, BWV 846-893

The Well-Tempered Clavier by Johann Sebastian Bach, first published in 1722 (Book I) and 1742 (Book II), is a landmark collection of preludes and fugues in all 24 major and minor keys. The work systematically explores the possibilities of equal temperament tuning, enabling music in every key. Each prelude and fugue pair demonstrates contrapuntal mastery and expressive depth. The collection influenced generations of composers and remains central to keyboard repertoire. Original manuscripts and early editions show Bach’s careful notation and occasional revisions, reflecting his meticulous compositional process.

This item is a handwritten manuscript copy of Das Wohltemperierte Klavier by Johann Sebastian Bach (1685–1750), catalogued under the shelfmark Mus.2405-T-7,1. The manuscript is preserved by the Sächsische Landesbibliothek – Staats- und Universitätsbibliothek Dresden (SLUB Dresden) and is available today as a digital object within the Königliche Privat-Musikaliensammlung digital collection. The document is dated approximately between 1760 and 1787, placing it after Bach’s death and identifying it clearly as an eighteenth-century copy rather than an autograph.
Das Wohltemperierte Klavier is one of the foundational works of Western keyboard music. It consists of a systematic collection of preludes and fugues written in all major and minor keys, demonstrating the possibilities of a well-tempered tuning system. This manuscript contains the first book of the work, corresponding to BWV 846–869, beginning with Praeludium I (BWV 846) and concluding with Praeludium XXIV (BWV 869). The internal structure of the manuscript reflects this ordered sequence of preludes, which are individually listed in the catalog record.
The physical extent of the item is described as an Abschrift (copy), and its structure type is identified as Handschrift (manuscript). The source language is marked as “not to be determined,” which is typical for instrumental music sources without extensive verbal text. The manuscript is associated with Dresden as its place of origin, though no specific copyist is identified.

Sketches for the prelude and act one of Tristan und Isolde by Richard Wagner

Piano scoring (sketch?) for first 19 bars of the prelude to act one, followed by various themes from that act. Tristan und Isolde (premiered 1865) is an opera by Richard Wagner that tells a tragic medieval love story. After Tristan kills Isolde’s fiancé in battle, he escorts her by ship to marry King Mark. On the voyage, they mistakenly drink a love potion and fall into an overwhelming and forbidden passion. Their love defies duty, loyalty, and social order, driving them toward death as the only realm where union is possible. Wagner’s revolutionary music, marked by unresolved harmonies and emotional intensity, made this opera a landmark of Western music, deeply influencing later composers and the development of modernism.

This item is a set of musical manuscript sketches related to the Prelude and the first act of Tristan und Isolde, an opera by Richard Wagner (1813-1883). The material was produced approximately between 1850 and 1859, during the period in which Wagner was working on the conception and composition of the opera. Unlike a finished score, these sketches represent preparatory work and document an early stage of the compositional process.
Tristan und Isolde is one of Wagner’s most important operas and a central work of nineteenth-century music drama. The Prelude and Act One introduce the main musical and dramatic ideas of the opera, which is based on the medieval legend of Tristan and Isolde and explores themes of love, desire, and fate. The manuscript sketches are therefore directly connected to the development of these core elements and to Wagner’s method of shaping music and drama together.
The composer associated with the manuscript is Richard Wagner, while Siegfried Wagner (1869–1930), his son, is listed as an associated name, likely in relation to the later history, ownership, or preservation of the material. The item is classified as a music manuscript and falls within the genre of scores.
The manuscript is held in the Music Division of the library and is stored in a locked case (Shelf locator: JOB 73–92), indicating its status as a rare and valuable object. As an original manuscript source connected to Tristan und Isolde, it is primarily of interest for musicological research, especially for the study of Wagner’s compositional practices and the history of the opera.

Tragic mask

Plaque depicting a female tragic theatrical mask with hair bound by a diadem. Tragic masks in ancient Greek theater were essential tools for performance, allowing actors to embody gods, heroes, and mythic figures while heightening emotional expression. Carved or molded with exaggerated features, wide, open mouths, deeply contoured brows, dramatic eyes, they made gestures and feelings visible even to spectators seated far from the stage.

This small bone plaque comes from late Republican Rome or the early decades of the Empire and depicts a female tragic mask, a central motif in ancient theatre. Masks, in both Greece and Rome, were used to make characters immediately recognizable and to convey strong emotions even to spectators seated far from the stage, turning the face into a universal symbol of pathos and drama. In this sense, tragedy was not merely entertainment, but a moment of reflection on the human condition, on fate, and on the passions that drive action.
The plaque, smaller than a real mask, has a pierced hole suggesting it was used decoratively on furniture or other domestic objects, rather than as an actual stage element. The female face, with gathered hair and a diadem, evokes mythological figures or tragic characters of high status, while the pronounced features – large eyes, downturned mouth, and arched eyebrows – make the suffering and pathos immediately perceptible. The material, carefully worked bone, demonstrates Roman craftsmanship in transforming a commonplace object into a cultural and theatrical symbol: in this way, a small domestic object condensed the essence of theatrical drama, bringing the grandeur and evocative power of the public stage into private life.

Comic mask

Comic theatrical mask from Magna Graecia, attributed to Italic farce, a popular and licentious genre. It represents the features of a man with a deformed, bald face. It comes from a cremation tomb and testifies to the strong connection between the world of the dead, entertainment, and Dionysian cults.

The mask is one of the most significant symbols of ancient Italy’s comic theatre. Italic farces — derived from the Greek phlyakes — were short popular performances of a satirical and grotesque nature, born in Southern Italy during the Magna Graecia period. Through comedy and the exaggeration of features, they staged everyday life, vices, fears, and human weaknesses, serving as an ironic mirror of society.
The mask, preserved in the National Archaeological Museum of Taranto (MArTA), is a terracotta artifact dating to around the 2nd–1st century BCE, originating from a funerary context in the area of Taranto, an ancient city of Magna Graecia. It was discovered during excavations near a necropolis, where it likely formed part of the burial goods of an actor or a theatre enthusiast. The piece depicts a highly expressive male face, with a wide-open mouth, pronounced nose, arched eyebrows, and bulging eyes — features typical of comic and farcical characters. The modelling is lively and deliberately exaggerated, intended to be recognizable even from a distance during performances. From a symbolic perspective, the mask reflects the connection between the world of the living and that of the dead: theatre, a place of fiction and transformation, becomes a metaphor for life itself, always suspended between reality and illusion. This unique artifact bears witness to the importance of the Italic farce as an artistic and social expression in Hellenistic Taranto, a city where Greek and local cultures blended to create one of antiquity’s most refined theatrical traditions.

Kylix with Apollo and kithara

Attic red-figure kylix (480-470 BC). The interior depicts Apollo, identifiable as the god of music, seated and playing the kithara, his symbolic instrument, beside a flaming altar. The exterior shows libation scenes with male and female figures, sceptres, and phialai, enriched with decorative motifs such as meanders and palmettes.

Red-figure kylix created circa 480 BC-470 BC. The interior depicts Apollo, beardless, with long wavy hair, a laurel wreath, and a himation around his legs, seated to the right on a diphros. The god plays a large kithara and holds a fluted phiale in his right hand. On the right, half-seen, is a flaming altar, shaped like an Ionic column with necking of egg pattern.
The exterior depicts a woman in a long-sleeved chiton, himation, and saccos, who moves to right with left hand on her hip, holding out in her right an oinochoe, as if to pour into a phiale which a bearded, wreathed man on right holds out to her. He has a long sceptre leaning against his left shoulder, and wears a long-sleeved talaric chiton and himation. On the left, a youth, wreathed, and wearing an himation, holding upright a sceptre in his left, and extending a fluted phiale in his right. Between him and the woman is a slender Ionic column with architrave; behind him hang a pair of sandals. On the left of the bearded man hang a pair of tablets rolled up, with a stylus fastened in the bands: at each of the lower corners hangs a triple tie.
On the other side, apparently the same three figures are engaged, but part of each is missing. Of the youth on left only the feet and the ends of his himation and the legs of the chair in which he is sitting to right are preserved. The woman confronts him, raising both hands apparently, with a gesture of surprise; she wears a fillet with trefoil ends; her face and right arm are missing. Behind her is the column as we can see on the other side. The bearded man on the right stands en face, but looking to left with right hand on hip, left holding sceptre upright; his sceptre is striated. On the right is a trace of an object (a sandal ?) hanging up, which is almost entirely broken away.
Purple tuning pegs of lyre, flames, wreaths in exterior, fillets, cords of tablets and of sandals. Eye in transition stage (in interior, the eyelids are in profile; the eyeball is drawn as a dot in a semicircle). Around the interior, a band of running maeander. Below the exterior, a thin red line. Below the handles a palmette. The exergue in the interior is left red.

Euripides' Bacchae Fragments

Fragments on which the beginning of the 1st line of Euripides' Bacchae has been written 3 or 4 times. Euripides’ Bacchae (late 5th century BCE) is a tragic drama centered on the arrival of Dionysus in Thebes and the city’s refusal to acknowledge his divinity. King Pentheus attempts to suppress the god’s cult, but Dionysus drives the women of Thebes into ecstatic frenzy and leads Pentheus to his brutal death at the hands of his own mother. The play explores themes of divine power, human arrogance, identity, and the tension between reason and instinct. Its blend of psychological depth, ritual, and violence has made it one of the most striking and unsettling works of Greek tragedy.

This item consists of a set of four fragmentary pieces of papyrus on which the opening words of line 1 of Bacchae by Euripides have been written three or four times. The text is in ancient Greek and preserves only a very small portion of the tragedy, focusing exclusively on the repeated copying of the same line rather than on continuous literary transmission.
The fragments form part of the Tebtunis Papyri collection and are held by The Bancroft Library. The material is classified as literary papyri, although its function appears to be primarily educational rather than performative or archival. The handwriting is described as a large “schoolboy hand,” which, together with the repetition of the same line, strongly suggests that the papyrus was used as a writing exercise, likely for the practice of penmanship.
Physically, the object survives in an incomplete state and consists of four papyrus fragments of various sizes. The text is written in a single column of four lines on the recto, along the papyrus fibers. The papyrus sheet had been reused: it was probably originally employed for an account or another documentary purpose, and traces of the earlier writing remain visible, particularly on the verso.
As a material object, the papyrus provides clear evidence of reuse and of everyday writing practices in antiquity. Rather than transmitting the Bacchae as a literary text, it documents the educational context in which canonical Greek authors such as Euripides were copied and memorized. The repetition of the opening line reflects the pedagogical importance of classical tragedy in Greek education and offers valuable insight into ancient schooling, handwriting training, and the secondary use of writing materials.
